# Writing Style Guide

## Core Principles

**Direct and substantive**: Get to the point quickly. Avoid introductory throat-clearing or hedging language. The first sentence should carry weight.

**Personal but not self-centered**: Use personal experience as evidence, not the main event. Frame discoveries as observations readers can verify themselves.

**Technical precision with natural flow**: Use exact terminology, but maintain conversational rhythm. Technical accuracy should not sacrifice readability.

## Sentence Construction

### Preferred Patterns

**Short declarative statements for impact:**

> Chrome is effectively everywhere you look. And that's bad news.

**Em dashes for contextual additions:**

> The browser comes preinstalled in most Android handsets sold outside of China, and serves as the UI layer of Chrome OS, Google's foray into desktop and tablet operating systems.

**Parallel structure for rhythm:**

> It's empowering, eye-opening, and above all, fun.

### Avoid

- Unnecessary qualifiers like "basically", "essentially", and "pretty much"
- Passive constructions when active voice works
- Question-as-transition devices like "So what does this mean?"
- Overuse of "I think" or "in my opinion"

## Paragraph Architecture

Typical paragraph flow:

1. Topic sentence with concrete detail
2. One to three supporting sentences
3. No summary sentence unless transitioning

Example:

> Within two years, Chrome accounted for 15% of all Web traffic on desktop, and for comparison, it took Firefox 6 years to get there. Google managed to deliver a fast, thoughtfully designed browser that was an instant hit among users and Web developers alike. Their product innovation and engineering prowess was a breath of fresh air, and their commitment to open-source was the cherry on top.

Paragraphs are usually two to four sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs are for emphasis or transition.

## Voice Characteristics

### First Person

Use first person for:

- Personal experiences
- Direct observations
- Recommendations grounded in experience

Avoid first person for:

- Obvious opinions
- Unnecessary hedging
- Inflating mundane actions

### Second Person

Appropriate:

> You might like it as a browser. And you might not mind the privacy trade-offs it comes with.

Avoid:

- Patronizing instructions
- Assumed agreement

## Technical Writing Style

### Code Examples

Keep code examples minimal and purposeful:

```css
display: grid;
grid-template-columns: repeat(auto-fill, minmax(20rem, 1fr));
grid-gap: 1rem;
```

Introduce code with concrete context:

> Here is a variable-free version of what I ended up with:

### Technical Explanations

Lead with the practical application:

> By default, git fetch gets an updated list of remote branches from the remote and sets up remote branch references locally. However, it doesn't delete stale references...

Avoid theory-first framing when a practical lead is clearer.

## Distinctive Elements

### Metaphors and Analogies

Use them sparingly and keep them concrete.

### Cultural and Historical Context

Integrate it naturally when relevant.

### Critical Perspective

Frame critiques with evidence. Avoid snark without substance, strawman arguments, and unqualified absolutism.

## Formatting Conventions

### Headings

Prefer action-oriented or topic-specific headings:

- `Setting up Colima`
- `Don't Be Evil`
- `Round 1: Basic Tasks`

Avoid generic headings like `Introduction`, `My Thoughts`, or `What I Found`.

### Lists

Minimize bullet points and integrate content into prose where possible. When lists are necessary, make each item substantive.

### Emphasis

Use italics for introduced terms, genuine emphasis, and foreign words. Avoid bold emphasis in prose, all caps, and multiple exclamation points.

## Topic-Specific Patterns

### Product Announcements

Lead with the announcement, then add context.

### Technical Guides

Use this shape:

1. Brief context
2. Core instructions
3. Practical outcomes or verification
4. Caveats or alternatives if relevant
