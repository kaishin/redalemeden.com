Alongside the [Kojiki](/blog/2006/06/japanese-mythology-origins/), the
_Nihonshoki_ (日本書紀), also called _Nihongi_ (日本紀), is the second most
important source of _Shinwa_. Compiled in 720 AD during the Nara period
(710–794) under Prince Toneri, it is more comprehensive than its predecessor.

The _Nihonshoki_ spans from the beginning of the world to the reign of Emperor
Jito in 697, divided into 30 chapters, one per emperor, except the first two
which cover the mythological era. Unlike the _Kojiki_, it was compiled from
multiple sources including Chinese and Korean Baekje texts, and often cites
different versions of the same events with their original attributions.

Both the virtues of good rulers and the failings of bad ones are recorded, which
gives it a more objective character than the _Kojiki_. Another key difference:
the _Kojiki_ was written in transliterated Japanese using Chinese characters,
while the _Nihonshoki_ was written in classical Chinese, as was standard for
official documents of the time.
