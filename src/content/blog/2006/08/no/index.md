_Nō_ (能), also romanized as _Nou_ or _Noh_, is one of three main Japanese
traditional performing arts, alongside _Kabuki_ and _Bunraku_. As the new season
of _Nō_ plays starts this month,
[Metropolis magazine](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://metropolis.co.jp/tokyo/recent/feature.asp)
has an informative article on its roots and characteristics. The
[Wikipedia entry](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noh)
has additional facts, and this
[glossary of _Nou_ terms](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://etext.lib.virginia.edu/japanese/noh/brazellgloss.html)
is useful for the uninitiated. The same site has several
[famous _Nou_ plays translated](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://etext.lib.virginia.edu/japanese/noh/index.html),
with the original Japanese transcript.

There is another theatrical form often associated with _Nō_ called
[_Kyogen_](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyogen).
It serves as intermission between _Noh_ acts, featuring short comical pieces
laced with satire and parody.
[This site](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://www2.ntj.jac.go.jp/unesco/noh/en/index.html)
covers the key differences between _Noh_ and _Kyogen_, along with some
[streamable videos](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://www2.ntj.jac.go.jp/unesco/noh/en/movie.html).

One remarkable feature of _Noh_ masks, called _Noumen_ (能面): they change
expression depending on the angle of view.
[This page](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://www.kasrl.org/noh_mask.html)
explains the visual illusion. For a closer look, there is an exhaustive
[list of _Nou_ masks](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://nohmask21.com/eu/mlist.html)
and a guide to their
[carving process](https://web.archive.org/web/20061107172136/http://nohmask21.com/eu/process.html).
